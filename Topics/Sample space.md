Set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment

### Examples of Sample Spaces

- Coin toss: $\Omega = \{H, T\}$
- Die roll: $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$
- Utterance with pressure measurement, quantised and stored: $\Omega = \{u_1, u_2, \ldots\}$
- Camera image (pixel store): $\Omega = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots\}$

A sample space must be exhaustive and exclusive. The term “exhaustive” means that the sample space has to cover all possible outcomes. If there is one possible outcome that is left out, then the set is no longer a sample space. The term “exclusive” means that the sample space contains unique elements so that there is no repetition of elements.

- A sample space Ω is the collection of all possible outcomes.
- The outcomes can be numbers, alphabets, vectors, or functions. The outcomes can also be images, videos, EEG signals, audio speeches, etc.
- ΩΩ must be exhaustive and exclusive.
